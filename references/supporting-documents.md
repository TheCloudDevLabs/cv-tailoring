# Supporting Documents

This reference covers the structure and approach for each type of supporting document that can be produced alongside a tailored CV.

## Cover letter

A cover letter should be a concise, one-page document that connects the candidate's experience to the specific role requirements. It should not restate the CV.

### Structure

**Opening paragraph.** Name the role being applied for and where it was advertised. Briefly state why the role is a good fit based on career background and current focus.

**Middle paragraph(s).** One or two paragraphs that address the most important requirements of the role, drawing on specific experience. Focus on the two or three strongest alignment points rather than trying to address everything. Use concrete examples where possible, but keep them brief. Reference the organisation or organisation by name where known.

**Closing paragraph.** Express availability and willingness to discuss further. Include contact details. Keep it short.

### Principles

- Do not open with "I am writing to apply for..." or similarly generic phrasing. Lead with substance.
- Do not repeat the CV. The cover letter should complement it by providing context, motivation, or emphasis that the CV format cannot convey.
- Match the tone to the organisation. Government roles typically expect formal but not stiff language.
- Reference security clearance early if the role requires it and you hold one.
- Keep the total length to one A4 page.

## Selection criteria response

A selection criteria response addresses each criterion individually with a structured narrative drawing on specific project examples.

### Structure per criterion

Each criterion response follows a narrative arc:

**Context.** Briefly set the scene. Name the organisation, programme, or project. Describe the challenge or requirement that created the need for the capability being assessed.

**Actions.** Describe what was done, with enough technical specificity to demonstrate genuine experience. Attribute actions honestly. If the work was collaborative, say so and describe the specific contribution.

**Outcome.** Describe the result. Where possible, connect the outcome to something measurable or meaningful to the organisation.

This is similar to a STAR format but should read as a natural narrative rather than labelled sections.

### Principles

- Each criterion response should be self-contained. Do not assume the reader has read previous criteria.
- Draw on specific projects from the employment history. Name the organisation, the platform, the problem, and the approach.
- Do not use generic statements like "I have extensive experience in..." without backing them up with specifics.
- Typical length: three to five paragraphs per criterion, depending on the complexity of the criterion.
- If a criterion asks for multiple capabilities, address each one with at least one specific example.
- Use the same writing standards as the CV: plain English, consistent spelling, no promotional language.

## Pitch document

A pitch document is a short summary (one to two pages) that presents the candidate's value proposition for a specific role or engagement. It is less formal than a selection criteria response and more direct than a CV.

### Structure

**Opening statement.** A brief paragraph positioning the candidate for the specific role. Name the role, the organisation, and the core capability being offered.

**Key alignment areas.** Two to four short sections, each covering a major area of alignment between the candidate's experience and the role requirements. Each section should have a clear heading and one to two paragraphs of supporting detail.

**Engagement approach.** A brief paragraph on how the candidate would approach the first 30 to 90 days, or the key priorities they would focus on. This should be practical and grounded in the specifics of the role.

### Principles

- Pitch documents are useful for contract roles or when engaging directly with a hiring manager rather than through a formal recruitment process.
- Keep it concise. The pitch should add value beyond the CV, not duplicate it.
- Focus on what the candidate brings to the specific situation, not general capabilities.
